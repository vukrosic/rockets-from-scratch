# Lesson 12: Orbital Delta-V

Spacecraft change orbit by changing velocity.

Delta-v is the language of those changes.

Why care about orbital delta-v?

Because once a spacecraft is in orbit, missions are planned as velocity changes:

```text
raise orbit
lower orbit
transfer
correct course
arrive
deorbit
```

Each action spends some delta-v.

## Step 1: Delta-V Is Mission Currency

If a mission needs a certain orbit change, it needs a certain delta-v budget.

That budget is spent by burns.

This is how orbital design is often discussed:

```text
how much delta-v does the mission need?
```

## Step 2: Prograde And Retrograde Burns

A prograde burn points along the direction of motion.

It usually raises orbital energy.

A retrograde burn points opposite the direction of motion.

It usually lowers orbital energy.

## Step 3: Orbit Shape Changes

Burns do not just make the craft "faster."

They change the shape of the orbit.

A prograde burn at one point can raise the opposite side of the orbit.

A retrograde burn can lower it.

## Step 4: Apoapsis And Periapsis

For an orbit around Earth:

```text
apoapsis:
    highest point

periapsis:
    lowest point
```

Different burns at different points change those heights in different ways.

## Step 5: Delta-V Budget Thinking

Mission planning often breaks the journey into parts:

```text
launch
orbit insertion
transfer
correction
arrival
reentry
```

Each phase uses some delta-v.

The sum is the total mission budget.

## Step 6: Tiny Hand Check

Question:

```text
What does a prograde burn usually do to the orbit?
```

Answer:

```text
raises orbital energy and changes orbit shape
```

## Step 7: Three Quick Questions

Question:

```text
What does a prograde burn point along?
```

Answer:

```text
the direction of motion
```

Question:

```text
What does a retrograde burn point against?
```

Answer:

```text
the direction of motion
```

Question:

```text
What is a delta-v budget?
```

Answer:

```text
the sum of velocity changes needed for mission phases
```
