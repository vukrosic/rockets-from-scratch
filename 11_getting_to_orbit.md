# Lesson 11: Getting To Orbit

Orbit is not just "going high."

Orbit is moving fast enough sideways that you keep falling around the planet
instead of hitting it.

## Step 1: The Orbit Idea

If you throw something forward, gravity pulls it downward.

If the forward speed is too small, it falls back.

If the speed is just right for the altitude, it keeps missing the ground.

That is the core orbit idea.

## Step 2: Horizontal Speed Matters

A rocket launch has two jobs:

```text
go up enough to clear the thick atmosphere
gain sideways speed
```

Altitude alone is not orbit.

Orbit needs significant horizontal velocity.

## Step 3: Circular Orbit Intuition

In a circular orbit, gravity provides the inward pull required to keep the
vehicle moving around Earth.

The vehicle is always falling, but the ground curves away underneath it.

That is why orbit feels like falling sideways.

## Step 4: Gravity Losses

If the rocket spends too long fighting gravity straight up, it wastes delta-v.

That wasted part is called gravity loss in simple mission language.

That is one reason launch trajectories bend gradually toward horizontal.

## Step 5: Drag Losses

Going through atmosphere also costs delta-v because drag steals energy and
momentum.

That is why rockets try to reduce drag during ascent.

## Step 6: Simple Orbit Picture

If a vehicle is at the right altitude and speed, it can stay in orbit.

If its speed is too low:

```text
it falls back
```

If its speed is too high:

```text
its orbit shape changes
```

## Step 7: Tiny Hand Check

Question:

```text
What makes orbit different from a high arc?
```

Answer:

```text
extra sideways speed
```
