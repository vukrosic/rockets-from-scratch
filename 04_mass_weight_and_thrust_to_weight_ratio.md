# Lesson 04: Mass, Weight, And Thrust-To-Weight Ratio

Rockets get lighter as propellant is used.

That changing mass is one of the biggest differences between rockets and many
other vehicles.

## Step 1: Mass Versus Weight

Mass is how much matter or inertia an object has.

Weight is the gravitational force on that mass:

```text
W = m g
```

Mass is measured in:

```text
kg
```

Weight is measured in:

```text
N
```

Example:

```text
mass = 100 kg
weight = 100 * 9.8 = 980 N
```

## Step 2: Wet Mass And Dry Mass

Wet mass means the vehicle plus all propellant.

Dry mass means the vehicle after propellant is gone.

Rocket mass decreases during flight because propellant leaves as exhaust.

That is why rockets can accelerate more later in the burn.

## Step 3: Thrust-To-Weight Ratio

Thrust-to-weight ratio compares engine thrust to vehicle weight:

```text
TWR = thrust / weight
```

If:

```text
TWR > 1
```

then thrust is larger than weight in the simple upward-liftoff picture.

If:

```text
TWR < 1
```

the rocket cannot lift off in that simple model.

## Step 4: Example

Suppose:

```text
thrust = 1500 N
weight = 1000 N
```

Then:

```text
TWR = 1500 / 1000 = 1.5
```

This suggests the rocket can lift off, assuming other losses are not dominant.

## Step 5: Why TWR Changes During Flight

As propellant burns:

```text
mass decreases
weight decreases
```

If thrust stays similar, TWR rises.

That means a rocket can start with one acceleration and later accelerate more
strongly.

## Step 6: Liftoff Is Not Just TWR

The real liftoff condition also has drag and other effects.

But as a first model:

```text
thrust > weight
```

is the key idea.

## Step 7: Tiny Hand Check

A rocket has:

```text
mass = 200 kg
thrust = 3000 N
```

Weight:

```text
W = 200 * 9.8 = 1960 N
```

TWR:

```text
TWR = 3000 / 1960 = 1.53
```
