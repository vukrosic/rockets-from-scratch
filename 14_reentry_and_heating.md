# Lesson 14: Reentry And Heating

Coming back to Earth is not just the reverse of going up.

Reentry is a high-energy atmospheric problem.

Why care about reentry?

Because reaching space is only half the problem if the mission has to return.

A spacecraft in orbit carries enormous kinetic energy.

Reentry is about managing where that energy goes.

## Step 1: Why Reentry Heats Things Up

A spacecraft in orbit has a lot of kinetic energy.

When it enters the atmosphere, drag converts some of that energy into heat and
motion of the air.

That is why reentry is a thermal design problem, not just a navigation problem.

## Step 2: Drag Again

The same drag idea from ascent matters during descent.

Drag opposes the motion.

As speed and atmospheric density increase, drag and heating can become severe.

## Step 3: Blunt Shapes

Reentry vehicles often use blunt shapes.

The goal is to control where the heating goes and how the air flows around the
vehicle.

We are not building one here.

We are learning the model:

```text
shape affects airflow
airflow affects heating
heating affects design
```

## Step 4: Entry Angle Matters

If the entry is too steep:

```text
too much heating too quickly
```

If the entry is too shallow:

```text
the vehicle may skip or stay in the atmosphere too long
```

So reentry requires careful trajectory planning.

## Step 5: Heat Shield Idea

A heat shield protects the vehicle by taking the thermal punishment.

That lets the rest of the spacecraft survive.

The shield is part of the thermal management model.

## Step 6: Tiny Hand Check

Question:

```text
Why is reentry not just "falling slowly"?
```

Answer:

```text
because orbital speed is very high, so drag converts a lot of kinetic energy into heat
```
